MENTOR GUIDE

Purpose: Guide for mentors assessing learner responses to scenarios Format: Detailed answer keys and assessment criteria

How to Use This Guide

Assessment Principles

What You're Evaluating:

  1. Diagnostic Reasoning - Do they think systematically or jump to conclusions?
  2. Technical Knowledge - Do they understand hardware components and failure patterns?
  3. Practical Judgment - Are recommendations appropriate for the context?
  4. Communication - Are explanations clear and actionable?
  5. Prevention Mindset - Do they think beyond the immediate fix?

Important Notes:

  • Look for sound reasoning more than "perfect" answers
  • There are often multiple valid approaches
  • Assess the process, not just the conclusion
  • Use scenarios to identify learning needs

Providing Feedback

For Strong Responses:

  • Acknowledge what they did well
  • Point out excellent reasoning or insights
  • Suggest minor refinements or additional considerations

For Adequate Responses:

  • Confirm what was correct
  • Highlight what they missed or could improve
  • Ask probing questions to develop their thinking
  • Point them to resources or concepts to study

For Weak Responses:

  • Identify where reasoning went wrong
  • Work through the scenario together
  • Focus on teaching the diagnostic process
  • Assign additional practice in areas of weakness

FOUNDATIONAL SCENARIOS - ANSWER GUIDE

F1: The Slow Startup - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Classic signs of a failing or severely fragmented hard drive (HDD), possibly complicated by power issues. The long boot time with normal operation afterward suggests storage is the bottleneck, not RAM or CPU.

Expected Diagnosis:

Primary: Hard drive issues (failing HDD or severely fragmented/full drive)

Secondary consideration: Possible corruption from power outages

Key Diagnostic Questions Learner Should Ask:

  • What type of storage does the computer have? (HDD vs SSD - HDDs are more susceptible)
  • How full is the hard drive?
  • Does she hear any clicking or grinding sounds during startup?
  • Has she noticed any error messages?
  • What happens during those 5-7 minutes? (Does she see the Windows logo, loading screen, etc.?)

Recommended Steps (Acceptable Answers):

  1. Check drive health using built-in diagnostics (CHKDSK) or manufacturer tools
  2. Check available storage space
  3. If HDD is failing: backup immediately, prepare for replacement
  4. If drive is just full/fragmented: free up space, defragment (if HDD)
  5. Consider SSD upgrade if replacement is needed

Preventive Advice Should Include:

  • Get a surge protector immediately (addresses the power outage risk)
  • Use UPS/battery backup if available
  • Regular backups (external drive or cloud)
  • Keep at least 15-20% of drive space free
  • Proper shutdown procedures (don't just close lid during power outage)

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Jumping to "needs more RAM" without asking diagnostic questions
  • Not recognizing the urgency of potential drive failure
  • Forgetting to emphasize backup before attempting fixes
  • Missing the power protection issue in preventive advice

Teaching Points:

  • Boot time problems usually indicate storage issues, not processing power
  • Power instability is a major hardware killerâ€”always address it
  • Backup before troubleshooting storage problems
  • SSDs are more reliable than HDDs in challenging environments

F2: The Black Screen - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Computer is powering on (fan runs, power light on) but no display. Most likely causes given the humidity context: 1) Display connection issue (possibly from condensation), 2) RAM not seated properly (can be caused by humidity/corrosion), or 3) Display hardware failure.

Expected Diagnosis:

Learner should recognize this is likely hardware-related (not software) since the computer powers on but produces no display. The humidity context is a critical clue.

Possible Causes (in Order of Likelihood):

  1. Loose or corroded display cable connection (from humidity)
  2. RAM issues (humidity causing poor connection)
  3. Display failure
  4. Graphics hardware problem (less likely)

Diagnostic Steps to Walk James Through:

1. Basic checks first:

  • Try external monitor if available (isolates display vs. graphics issue)
  • Listen for beep codes
  • Check for indicator lights (hard drive activity, etc.)

2. If learner is comfortable guiding hardware steps:

  • Power down completely, unplug, remove battery
  • Let computer sit and dry for several hours
  • Reseat RAM (remove and reinstall memory modules)
  • Restart and check

3. If uncertain or problem persists:

  • Document symptoms clearly
  • Escalate to IT support
  • Consider environmental solutions (dehumidifier, sealed storage)

Recommended Action (Acceptable Answers):

  • Short term: Let computer dry completely before attempting restart
  • If comfortable: Guide James through reseating RAM
  • If persists: External monitor test, then likely needs professional attention
  • Document everything for escalation

Environmental Precautions:

  • Store laptop in sealed container with silica gel packets when not in use
  • Allow computer to acclimate to room temperature before powering on (if moved from different environment)
  • Keep workspace as dry as possible
  • Don't work immediately after moving from cold/humid environment
  • Consider dehumidifier if available

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Not connecting humidity context to the problem
  • Attempting too complex repairs remotely
  • Not suggesting the "let it dry" approach
  • Recommending software troubleshooting for clearly hardware issue

Teaching Points:

  • Power-on with no display = hardware issue, not software
  • Environmental factors (humidity, temperature change) cause many hardware problems
  • Sometimes "wait and let it dry" is the right first step
  • External monitor is valuable diagnostic tool
  • Know when to escalate rather than troubleshoot further

Acceptable Variations:

  • Some learners may be more conservative (escalate immediately) - that's fine for this scenario
  • Others may be more hands-on (guide RAM reseating) - also acceptable if they emphasize safety and James's comfort level
  • Both approaches are valid; assess their reasoning, not their boldness

F3: The Charging Problem - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Battery charging issue, likely caused by: 1) Aged battery (2 years is typical lifespan, especially in hot environments), 2) Power adapter failure from voltage fluctuations, or 3) Charging port/circuitry damage. Heat accelerates battery degradation significantly.

Expected Diagnosis:

Learner should identify this as potentially multiple hardware components and recognize the need to isolate which component is failing.

Likely Causes (All Plausible):

  1. Battery degradation (most likely given age + heat)
  2. Power adapter failure (voltage fluctuations damage adapters)
  3. Charging port issues (loose connection, damage)
  4. Motherboard charging circuitry (less common, but possible)

Diagnostic Process to Determine Cause:

Ask Priya to check:

  1. Does the charging light behave normally? (If flickering/inconsistent = adapter or port issue)
  2. Try wiggling the charging cable - does connection seem loose? (Port problem)
  3. Check battery health in OS (Windows: Battery Report; most OSes have built-in tools)
  4. If possible, try different adapter (isolates adapter vs. battery/port)
  5. Check adapter brick temperature - is it excessively hot? (Failing adapter)

Immediate Recommendations (Acceptable Answers):

1. If battery is degraded but adapter works:

  • Can continue using plugged in (essentially desktop mode)
  • Budget for battery replacement
  • Reduce heat exposure to extend remaining life

2. If adapter is failing:

  • Replace adapter (more urgent - without it, can't work)
  • Use surge protector/voltage regulator with new adapter

3. If port is damaged:

  • May need professional repair
  • Extremely careful with cable (don't stress the connection)
  • Consider USB-C charging if laptop supports it (alternative port)

4. General immediate advice:

  • Keep laptop plugged in for now (if adapter partially works)
  • Backup data (in case of sudden failure)
  • Order replacement part based on diagnosis

Long-Term Preventive Measures:

  • Voltage stabilizer/surge protector (CRITICAL - should be emphasized)
  • UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) if available/affordable
  • Temperature management:
    • Work in coolest part of day if possible
    • Elevate laptop for airflow
    • Don't work with laptop on bed/soft surfaces that block vents
    • Consider laptop cooling pad
  • Battery care:
    • Don't leave plugged in 24/7 in extreme heat (if battery is healthy)
    • Partial discharge cycles better than full discharge
    • Store in cool place when not in use
  • Regular voltage check - if power is frequently outside normal range (check with multimeter if available), prioritize voltage protection

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Assuming it's only the battery without considering other causes
  • Not recognizing the urgency of adapter failure
  • Missing the voltage fluctuation risk in preventive advice
  • Recommending battery replacement without proper diagnosis
  • Not connecting heat to accelerated battery degradation

Teaching Points:

  • Multiple components involved in charging - isolate the problem
  • Heat is a major battery killer (environmental context matters)
  • Voltage fluctuations damage adapters and shorten component life
  • Surge protector/voltage regulator is essential infrastructure, not optional
  • Batteries have limited lifespan (2-4 years typical, less in heat)
  • Can often continue working plugged in even with dead battery
  • Some problems need replacement parts - help user prioritize

Acceptable Variations:

  • Conservative: Recommend testing before replacement (good)
  • Proactive: Suggest replacement battery and voltage protection (also good)
  • Either approach is fine if reasoning is sound
  • Look for: systematic diagnosis, appropriate urgency, strong prevention focus

INTERMEDIATE SCENARIOS - ANSWER GUIDE

I1: The Intermittent Shutdown - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Classic overheating problem. Computer shuts down to protect itself when CPU/GPU reaches critical temperature. Dust accumulation blocks cooling vents, especially problematic when running demanding software. More common after months without maintenance.

Expected Diagnosis:

Primary hypothesis: Overheating due to dust accumulation in cooling system

Alternative possibilities:

  • Failing power adapter (less likely - usually more consistent symptoms)
  • Battery issues (less likely - would see warnings)
  • RAM problems (less likely - would see blue screens/errors, not clean shutdowns)
  • Software issues (less likely given the random timing and correlation with heavy use)

Key Diagnostic Questions/Tests:

1. Confirm overheating:

  • Is fan running loud before shutdown?
  • Does the laptop feel very hot (especially near vents)?
  • Download temperature monitoring software (HWMonitor, Core Temp) - what temperatures right before shutdown?
  • Does shutdown happen more with processor-intensive tasks?

2. Check power-related alternatives:

  • Does it happen on battery vs. plugged in? (If only plugged in = adapter)
  • Is adapter excessively hot?

3. Environmental correlation:

  • Time of day pattern? (Hotter afternoon = more shutdowns?)
  • Position of laptop? (On desk vs. lap, blocked vents?)

Recommended Solution:

1. Immediate action:

  • Clean cooling vents externally (compressed air if available, or gentle brush)
  • Ensure laptop is on hard, flat surface with good airflow
  • Elevate slightly for better ventilation
  • Close unnecessary programs to reduce heat generation

2. If accessible and David is comfortable:

  • Open bottom panel and clean dust from fan and heat sink
  • Check that fan spins freely
  • Consider replacing thermal paste if very old (may require escalation)

3. If problem persists after cleaning:

  • Fan may be failing (would need replacement)
  • Heat sink may be damaged
  • Escalate for hardware assessment

Maintenance Routine to Establish:

  • Monthly: External vent cleaning with compressed air/brush
  • Quarterly: More thorough internal cleaning (if comfortable opening case)
  • Ongoing:
    • Keep work surface clear and clean
    • Never block vents (no working on bed/cushions)
    • Monitor fan noise (loud = working hard, should investigate why)
    • Shut down properly (not sleep) at end of day in dusty environment
  • Environmental:
    • Work in coolest available location
    • Close windows/doors during dust storms if possible
    • Consider air purifier if available

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Not recognizing the environmental context (dry season, dust, no AC) as key diagnostic clue
  • Jumping to complex hardware failure without considering cleaning first
  • Not asking about temperature/fan behavior
  • Missing the correlation with heavy software use
  • Not providing specific cleaning guidance
  • Forgetting to mention ongoing maintenance

Teaching Points:

  • Unexpected shutdowns without warning = thermal protection activating
  • Dust is the enemy of computers (blocks cooling)
  • Environmental context is diagnostic gold
  • Simple maintenance (cleaning) prevents major problems
  • Pattern recognition: "months without maintenance + dust + heavy use + shutdown" = overheating
  • Remote support means empowering users to do basic maintenance themselves
  • Always start with simplest explanation before assuming hardware failure

What If Problem Persists:

Learner should mention that if cleaning doesn't resolve it:

  • Fan might be failing (mechanical failure)
  • Thermal paste might need replacement (more advanced)
  • Heat sink could be damaged or improperly seated
  • Would escalate to IT support or consider replacement

This shows they understand the diagnostic hierarchy and know their limits.

I2: The Mysterious Beeping - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Three beeps typically indicate RAM issue (varies by manufacturer, but RAM is most common). Given context of rough transport, RAM module likely became unseated or shifted. This is actually one of the more fixable hardware issues remotely.

Expected Diagnosis:

Beep codes indicate: Hardware initialization failure, most commonly RAM

Given transport context: RAM module displaced/unseated during rough travel

Most likely specific cause: RAM not properly seated in slot (shock/vibration from transport)

Alternative possibilities:

  • RAM module damaged (less likely, but possible)
  • Other component unseated (less common)
  • Motherboard damage from impact (less likely, would likely show other symptoms)

What to Ask Sarah to Check/Try:

Phase 1 - No disassembly (start here):

  1. Try these "reset" steps:

    • Power off completely
    • Remove power adapter and battery (if removable)
    • Hold power button for 30 seconds (drains residual power)
    • Reconnect battery and power, try starting again

  2. Check for physical damage:

    • Any visible cracks or damage to case?
    • Any loose parts rattling inside?
    • Screen intact?

Phase 2 - If comfortable with basic disassembly:

  1. Reseat the RAM:
    • Power off, remove battery and power
    • Locate RAM access panel (usually bottom of laptop)
    • Remove RAM module(s) and reseat firmly
    • Ensure they click into place
    • Reassemble and test

Phase 3 - If Sarah is NOT comfortable or problem persists:

  1. Alternative approaches:
    • If she can reach someone with tech experience, have them reseat RAM
    • If not, this likely needs professional attention
    • Consider whether she has access to IT support or needs to send for repair

Recommended Response (Acceptable Answers Vary by Approach):

Conservative approach (appropriate):

  • Explain beep codes generally indicate hardware seating issue
  • Try the no-disassembly reset steps first
  • If those don't work, recommend finding local tech help to reseat RAM
  • This is fixable but may need hands-on assistance

More hands-on approach (also appropriate if well-explained):

  • Walk Sarah through reseating RAM with very clear, step-by-step instructions
  • Emphasize:
    • Take photos before disassembling (to remember how it goes back)
    • Work on non-static surface
    • Be gentle but firm
    • Only proceed if she feels comfortable
  • If she's uncertain, fallback to conservative approach

Both approaches are valid - assess based on:

  • Do they explain the reasoning clearly?
  • Do they gauge user comfort level?
  • Do they provide appropriate safety caveats?
  • Do they have a backup plan if initial approach fails?

Transport Advice for Future:

Laptop protection:

  • Padded laptop sleeve/case (essential)
  • Store in carry-on or bag with padding, not loose in backpack
  • Position in center of bag, surrounded by soft items
  • Remove before travel if possible (some equipment is too valuable to risk)

If must transport regularly:

  • Consider ruggedized case
  • Power down completely before transport (not just sleep mode)
  • Remove any external devices (USB drives, etc.)
  • Secure with straps inside bag so it doesn't shift

Audio equipment protection:

  • Similar principles - padding, secure storage
  • Keep in separate compartments
  • Consider pelican-style cases for valuable recording equipment
  • Test everything after transport before depending on it for work

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Not recognizing beep codes as diagnostic information
  • Missing the connection between rough transport and unseated RAM
  • Either being too hands-off (not offering reseat solution) or too hands-on (not checking user comfort)
  • Not providing safety caveats about working inside laptop
  • Forgetting to ask about removable battery (step 1 requires this)
  • Missing opportunity to educate about transport protection

Teaching Points:

  • Beep codes are the computer's way of reporting hardware problems
  • Different manufacturers use different codes (good to look up specific model)
  • RAM is one of the most easily dislodged components during transport
  • Reseating RAM is one of the simpler hardware fixes
  • Always assess user's comfort level before guiding hardware procedures
  • Good transport practices prevent many hardware issues
  • Having a photo documentation strategy helps with reassembly

What Good Answers Include:

  • Recognition that this is likely fixable
  • Appropriate assessment of user capability
  • Clear escalation path if first approach fails
  • Both immediate fix and preventive advice
  • Acknowledgment that remote hardware troubleshooting has limits

I3: The Performance Puzzle - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Multiple related issues that learners need to untangle:

  1. Disk full - directly causes slowness and freezing (primary issue)
  2. Overheating - secondary issue, possibly caused by #1 (system working harder) or separate dust/cooling issue
  3. Large audio files - explains why disk filled up unexpectedly

These issues compound each other. Full disk leads to more disk thrashing leads to more heat generation plus poor performance. Heat leads to thermal throttling leads to more slowness.

Expected Response - Prioritization:

Good answers will recognize these issues are interconnected and prioritize addressing them systematically. There are different valid approaches:

Approach A - Address disk space first:

  1. Disk full (immediate performance impact, fixable quickly)
  2. Cooling/heat (may improve once disk issue resolved, or may need separate attention)
  3. Long-term data management

Approach B - Parallel approach:

  1. Quick cooling fixes while also addressing disk space
  2. Both impact performance, can tackle simultaneously

Both are acceptable if well-reasoned.

Which Symptoms Are Related:

Learners should identify:

  • Related: Disk full + slow performance + freezing (directly connected)
  • Potentially related: Heat + slow performance (heat causes throttling, OR full disk causes more heat, OR both are separate issues compounding each other)
  • Key insight: Full disk makes everything worse, including heat generation

Diagnostic Approach:

Step 1 - Assess disk situation (most urgent):

  • How full is the drive? (exact numbers matter)
  • What's taking up the space? (use disk analyzer tool)
  • Where are the audio files stored?
  • How much total audio data has he collected?
  • Are there duplicates or unnecessary files?

Step 2 - Assess cooling situation:

  • Where exactly is it hot? (bottom near CPU/GPU area?)
  • Is fan running? How loud?
  • Are vents blocked or dusty?
  • What's the working environment like right now?
  • Use temperature monitoring software

Step 3 - Understand the workflow:

  • How is he managing audio files currently?
  • Does he have backup/archive system?
  • What's his typical data generation rate?
  • Does he need all audio files on the laptop all the time?

Solutions for Each Issue:

Disk Space (immediate actions):

1. Emergency space clearing:

  • Empty recycle bin/trash
  • Clear browser cache and temp files
  • Run Disk Cleanup (Windows) or equivalent
  • Move audio files to external storage (most important)

2. Systematic organization:

  • Transfer completed projects to external drive
  • Keep only active projects on laptop
  • Regular archiving schedule
  • Consider cloud backup if internet available

3. Prevent recurrence:

  • Monitor disk space weekly
  • Set up automatic warnings at 80% full
  • Establish data management workflow from day one of projects

Overheating (immediate actions):

1. Quick fixes:

  • Clean vents with compressed air/brush
  • Ensure laptop on hard, flat surface
  • Work in coolest part of day if possible
  • Close unnecessary programs

2. Assessment:

  • Check if heat improves after freeing disk space
  • Monitor temperatures after fixes
  • If still overheating, likely dust buildup or failing fan

3. Maintenance:

  • Regular cleaning schedule (monthly in humid/dusty environment)
  • Proper workspace setup
  • Consider laptop stand for better airflow

Long-term Workflow Solutions:

  • External hard drive for audio archive (primary solution)
  • Cloud storage for backups if connectivity allows
  • Data management SOPs: active projects on laptop, completed projects archived
  • Regular data offload schedule (weekly or after each project)
  • Larger capacity laptop if this is ongoing workflow issue

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Treating these as completely separate problems (missing the connections)
  • Addressing only one issue without considering the others
  • Not recognizing audio file workflow as the root cause of disk space issue
  • Jumping to hardware fixes without addressing obvious disk space problem first
  • Not providing long-term workflow solution (this will happen again!)
  • Missing the humidity factor for both storage and cooling concerns
  • Not asking diagnostic questions before recommending solutions

Teaching Points:

  • Multiple symptoms don't always mean multiple unrelated problems
  • Full disk causes cascading performance issues
  • Different types of work (audio recording) have different data management needs
  • Prevention is workflow design - help users set up sustainable practices
  • Environmental factors (humidity) affect both storage media and cooling
  • Remote locations need especially good data management (limited ability to add storage quickly)
  • Sometimes the "hardware problem" is actually a workflow/usage problem

What Strong Answers Include:

  • Recognition that issues are interconnected
  • Systematic prioritization with clear reasoning
  • Both immediate fixes AND long-term workflow solutions
  • Appropriate questions to fully understand the situation
  • Acknowledgment that this is predictable given the workflow (audio collection)
  • Emphasis on preventing recurrence through better data management
  • Sensitivity to resource constraints (external drives may not be immediately available)

Red Flags in Answers:

  • Only addressing one symptom
  • Recommending hardware replacement without trying fixes first
  • Not connecting the audio work to the disk space issue
  • Missing the urgency of the disk space problem
  • Overly complex solutions when simple ones will work
  • Not thinking about the sustainable workflow

COMPLEX SCENARIOS - ANSWER GUIDE

C1: The Cascade of Problems - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Severe power surge caused multiple component failures. This is a hardware emergency requiring immediate decision-making, not troubleshooting.

Component Damage Analysis:

Learners should identify multiple damaged components:

1. Power system damaged:

  • "Not charging" after surge = power adapter and/or charging circuitry damaged
  • Power light briefly on then off = power delivery failure

2. Storage drive failure:

  • Clicking sound = mechanical failure of hard drive (classic "click of death")
  • This is CRITICAL - data may be unrecoverable

3. Possible motherboard/other damage:

  • Burning odor = electrical component failure (extremely serious)
  • Could be power circuitry, capacitors, or other motherboard components

4. Multiple points of failure:

  • These symptoms together suggest surge traveled through multiple components
  • Not a single repairable issue - this is catastrophic hardware failure

Should Lisa Troubleshoot Further?

NO - and strong answers will clearly state this with reasoning:

Why not:

  • Burning odor indicates serious electrical damage - safety risk
  • Multiple major components failed - not economically repairable
  • Clicking hard drive = data loss risk if powered on repeatedly
  • Time constraint (work due in 1 week) - troubleshooting would waste precious time
  • Remote location - even if diagnosed, parts unavailable
  • Risk of additional damage if attempting to use damaged computer

This is a decision-making scenario, not a fixing scenario. Learners need to recognize when to STOP troubleshooting and move to mitigation.

Recommended Action Plan:

Strong answers will provide a prioritized, practical plan:

Immediate (Day 1):

1. DO NOT attempt to power on the computer again

  • Risk of additional damage
  • Risk of data corruption if drive briefly spins up
  • Safety risk with electrical damage

2. Assess data backup status:

  • When was last backup?
  • What data exists only on this computer?
  • What's the critical work that's due?

3. Contact the other language worker (2 hours away):

  • Explain situation
  • Ask about borrowing equipment
  • Assess what work can be done on borrowed computer

4. Notify stakeholders about situation:

  • Project deadline may need extension
  • Explain circumstances (power surge, equipment failure)
  • Outline plan to complete work

Short-term (Days 2-3):

5. Secure alternative computing:

  • Borrow from nearby colleague if possible
  • Restore from backup to borrowed machine
  • Prioritize completing critical work

6. Attempt data recovery (only if critical):

  • DO NOT attempt on her own
  • Clicking hard drive requires professional data recovery
  • In remote location, this may not be feasible
  • May need to send drive to city (4 hours away) if data is irreplaceable
  • Cost-benefit analysis: is data worth the time/expense?

7. Request replacement computer:

  • Document the damage
  • Submit replacement request
  • May take weeks/months to arrive

Long-term resolution:

8. Complete work on borrowed equipment 9. Receive and set up replacement computer 10. Implement preventive measures (see below)

Systemic Changes to Recommend:

Strong answers will emphasize prevention because this is avoidable:

Essential (highest priority):

1. Surge protector - NON-NEGOTIABLE

  • This entire situation was preventable
  • Should be standard equipment for all language workers
  • Not optional in areas with unstable power

2. Backup strategy - equally critical:

  • Automated daily backups (if possible)
  • Multiple backup locations (external drive + cloud if connectivity allows)
  • Test restores regularly (backups are useless if they don't work)
  • Never let backups get more than 24-48 hours old

3. Unplug during storms:

  • Standard practice: when storm approaches, save work and unplug
  • Surge protector helps but isn't foolproof against direct lightning strikes
  • This should be organizational policy

Additional protective measures:

4. Voltage regulator/stabilizer (if power fluctuates regularly)

5. UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) if available/affordable

  • Provides both surge protection and battery backup
  • Allows safe shutdown during outages

Organizational level:

6. Equipment insurance or rapid replacement program

  • Remote workers need fast access to backup equipment
  • Consider keeping spare laptops at regional hubs
  • Equipment failure shouldn't mean week-long work stoppages

7. Standard remote work setup requirements:

  • Surge protection mandatory
  • Backup systems mandatory
  • Training on storm protocols
  • Regular equipment audits

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Suggesting troubleshooting steps (wrong approach for this scenario)
  • Not recognizing the severity (multiple failed components)
  • Missing the safety aspect (burning odor = electrical hazard)
  • Not addressing the data recovery urgency
  • Focusing only on fixing current situation without prevention
  • Not considering the timeline constraint (work due in 1 week)
  • Not thinking about organizational/systemic solutions
  • Treating this as "bad luck" instead of preventable situation

Teaching Points:

  • Some situations require immediate action, not diagnosis
  • Multiple simultaneous failures = catastrophic, not repairable
  • Burning smell = electrical damage = safety issue, stop immediately
  • Time constraints affect decision-making (troubleshooting vs. alternative solutions)
  • Data loss risk increases with each power-on attempt of failing drive
  • Prevention (surge protection, backups) is vastly cheaper than recovery
  • Remote work requires redundancy and protection
  • Good consultants think beyond the immediate fix to systemic improvements
  • Sometimes the job is damage control, not repair

What Exceptional Answers Include:

  • Clear recognition this is beyond repair
  • Immediate data protection concerns
  • Practical short-term alternatives (borrowing equipment)
  • Timeline-aware planning
  • Strong emphasis on prevention with specific equipment recommendations
  • Organizational-level thinking (this affects others too)
  • Safety awareness (electrical damage risk)
  • Both tactical (what Lisa should do tomorrow) and strategic (how to prevent future occurrences) thinking

This Scenario Tests:

  • Decision-making under pressure
  • Knowing when NOT to troubleshoot
  • Prioritization skills
  • Practical problem-solving beyond just hardware knowledge
  • Prevention mindset
  • Systems thinking

It's one of the most important real-world skills: recognizing when the computer is a lost cause and pivoting to protecting data and continuing work.

C2: The Gradual Decline - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

Progressive hardware failure showing classic escalation pattern. The timeline reveals deteriorating system stability, most likely due to:

Primary hypothesis: Overheating causing cascading failures

  • Dust accumulation leads to poor cooling leads to thermal stress on components leads to deterioration over time
  • Heat causes: restarts (thermal protection), software crashes (CPU throttling/instability), display issues (GPU stress), port/connection issues (motherboard expansion due to heat cycles)

Alternative hypothesis: Failing motherboard or power system

  • Could explain the diverse symptoms
  • Less likely as primary cause but possible

Pattern Recognition:

Strong answers will identify the progression pattern:

  1. Intermittent software issues (least serious)
  2. More frequent crashes (escalating)
  3. Display problems (hardware beginning to show stress)
  4. Connection/port failures (hardware degradation advancing)
  5. System error messages (system recognizing hardware malfunction)

This is a deterioration cascade, not random unrelated problems.

What the Pattern Suggests:

  • Heat-stressed components gradually failing
  • OR motherboard/power system slowly degrading
  • NOT: coincidental multiple problems
  • NOT: software issues (pattern is wrong for software)

Critical Insight Learners Should Identify:

The pattern is accelerating. Problems are:

  • More frequent
  • More severe
  • Affecting more systems

This indicates imminent complete failure.

Urgency Assessment:

HIGH URGENCY - Strong answers will clearly state this.

Why urgent:

1. Data loss risk is increasing:

  • Hard drive may be next to fail
  • External drive not recognized = can't do current backup
  • 10 days since last backup = unacceptable risk level
  • Each day increases data loss risk

2. Complete failure likely imminent:

  • Acceleration pattern suggests "final failure" approaching
  • Could fail completely tomorrow or next week
  • Waiting risks losing everything

3. Time to replace is 2-3 months:

  • If he requests replacement today, still waiting months

C3: The Diagnostic Challenge - Answer Guide

What's Happening:

This scenario is deliberately ambiguous and could be either hardware OR software (or both). It tests whether learners can think through multiple hypotheses and design systematic diagnostic processes.

Possible Explanations:

HARDWARE possibilities:

1. Failing touchpad:

  • Touchpad becoming hypersensitive or registering phantom touches
  • Temperature changes affect touchpad calibration
  • Could explain cursor jumping and "touches" when not touching
  • "Worse in morning when cold" supports this (hardware behaves differently at different temperatures)

2. Touchpad palm rejection failure:

  • Not detecting palms vs. intentional touches
  • Could cause cursor jumping while typing
  • But doesn't explain cursor moving when hands completely off

3. Physical damage or moisture in touchpad:

  • Temperature extremes could cause condensation
  • Morning cold then warming up leads to condensation
  • Could create false inputs

4. Keyboard or touchpad cable issue:

  • Loose connection affected by temperature changes
  • Could cause erratic behavior
  • Temperature cycling (cold night, warm day) stresses connections

5. Motherboard issue:

  • Less likely but possible
  • Temperature-dependent electrical problems

SOFTWARE possibilities:

1. Malware (despite antivirus):

  • Remote access trojan (RAT)
  • Could explain cursor moving and programs opening "on their own"
  • Antivirus isn't perfect
  • "Things starting on their own" is classic RAT behavior
  • BUT: "worse in morning" doesn't fit malware pattern well

2. Software conflict or driver issue:

  • Touchpad driver corrupted or conflicting
  • Recent Windows update causing problems
  • Software behaving strangely at startup (timing issue)

3. Accessibility feature accidentally enabled:

  • Mouse Keys or similar feature
  • Could cause unexpected cursor behavior
  • Less likely given description

4. Background process interfering:

  • Some program taking control at specific times
  • Scheduled task running in morning
  • Could explain timing pattern

BOTH (hardware + software):

5. Hardware problem triggering software response:

  • Touchpad malfunction causing OS to misinterpret inputs
  • Best fit for all symptoms

Systematic Diagnostic Process:

Strong answers will design a methodical elimination process:

Phase 1 - Gather more information (Questions to ask Kenji):

1. Clarify the "on its own" behavior:

  • What exactly happens? (cursor just moves, or clicks happen too?)
  • Which programs open?
  • Does it happen at specific times or randomly?
  • Does it ever happen when he's watching but not touching anything?

2. Temperature correlation details:

  • What's the morning temperature vs. during day?
  • Does behavior improve as computer warms up, or stay bad all day?
  • Has he noticed condensation on or around computer?

3. Timing patterns:

  • Only first thing in morning, or other times too?
  • How long after starting computer does it begin?
  • Does restarting help or make it worse?

4. Recent changes:

  • Any new software installed?
  • Any Windows updates recently?
  • Any physical incidents (drops, spills)?
  • Working in new location?

5. Antivirus and security:

  • What antivirus? When last updated?
  • Full scan recently?
  • Any security warnings ever?

Phase 2 - Simple tests (non-invasive):

6. Disable touchpad test:

  • External USB mouse + completely disable touchpad in settings
  • If problems stop then touchpad hardware issue
  • If problems continue then NOT touchpad (malware or other cause)
  • This is the key diagnostic test

7. Safe Mode test:

  • Boot into Safe Mode
  • Does behavior occur in Safe Mode?
  • If NO then software/driver issue
  • If YES then more likely hardware

8. Temperature test:

  • Let computer warm to room temperature before starting
  • Does it change behavior?
  • Work in warmer location one morning - does it help?

9. Touchpad settings check:

  • Adjust sensitivity settings
  • Disable tap-to-click
  • Change palm rejection settings
  • Does any setting change the behavior?

Phase 3 - Software investigation (if touchpad test suggests software):

10. Malware scan with multiple tools:

  • Second opinion scan (Malwarebytes or similar)
  • Check startup programs
  • Review recent software installations
  • Check browser extensions

11. Driver investigation:

  • Update touchpad drivers
  • Roll back recent driver updates
  • Check for Windows updates

12. Process monitoring:

  • Watch what processes run in morning
  • Check scheduled tasks
  • Monitor network activity

Phase 4 - Hardware investigation (if tests suggest hardware):

13. Physical inspection:

  • Look for condensation, especially in morning
  • Check for touchpad damage
  • Feel for unusual heat in touchpad area

14. Environmental controls:

  • Store computer in warmer location overnight
  • Use sealed bag with desiccant if condensation suspected
  • Eliminate temperature cycling

Solution Based on Most Likely Cause:

If touchpad hardware issue (most likely given symptoms):

Immediate solutions:

  • Disable built-in touchpad completely
  • Use external USB mouse permanently
  • Adjust storage to minimize temperature cycling

Long-term solutions:

  • Touchpad replacement (if available/affordable)
  • Environmental controls (warmer storage, less temperature swing)
  • Continue with external mouse (practical workaround)

Why this is most likely:

  • Temperature correlation (hardware affected by cold)
  • Cursor behavior even when not typing (rules out palm rejection)
  • "Worse in morning" fits hardware temperature sensitivity
  • Mountain location = extreme temperature cycling

If malware (less likely but possible):

Immediate:

  • Full system scan with multiple tools
  • Check all startup programs
  • Review installed software
  • Change all passwords
  • Disconnect from network while investigating

If confirmed malware:

  • Full system cleanup or reinstall
  • Restore from clean backup if available
  • Review security practices

Alternative Hypothesis if Initial Diagnosis Wrong:

Strong answers will show contingency thinking:

"If disabling the touchpad doesn't resolve the cursor movement, then we're likely looking at malware despite the antivirus. The next step would be intensive malware investigation with multiple scanning tools and process monitoring. The temperature correlation might be coincidental, or the malware might be triggered by specific startup conditions that happen to occur more in the morning."

OR:

"If malware scans come back clean and the external mouse also shows strange behavior, we'd need to investigate motherboard or system-level issues, which would likely require professional diagnosis or replacement."

Common Mistakes to Watch For:

  • Jumping to conclusion without systematic investigation
  • Not designing an elimination process
  • Missing the touchpad disable test (key diagnostic step)
  • Not considering temperature as genuine hardware factor
  • Dismissing malware too quickly because antivirus is running
  • Not having contingency plan for wrong initial diagnosis
  • Overcomplicated diagnosis when simple test (disable touchpad) would clarify
  • Not thinking about practical workarounds (external mouse)

Teaching Points:

  • Ambiguous symptoms require systematic elimination, not guessing
  • Design tests that clearly distinguish between hypotheses
  • Environmental factors (temperature) genuinely affect hardware
  • Simple tests (disable touchpad) can quickly narrow possibilities
  • Always have "if I'm wrong" backup hypothesis
  • Good diagnosis is about process, not jumping to answers
  • Some problems have simple workarounds even if root cause unclear
  • Remote diagnosis means designing tests user can perform
  • Multiple possible explanations = need decision tree, not single answer

What Exceptional Answers Include:

  • Clear acknowledgment that this could be multiple things
  • Well-structured diagnostic decision tree
  • Specific test that distinguishes hardware from software (touchpad disable)
  • Questions to gather more information before recommending tests
  • Recognition of temperature as legitimate diagnostic clue
  • Practical workaround (external mouse) while investigating
  • Contingency thinking ("if this doesn't work, then...")
  • Both hardware and software investigation paths
  • Appropriate level of skepticism about antivirus (not foolproof)
  • Environmental context (mountain location, temperature extremes) informing diagnosis

This Scenario Tests:

  • Tolerance for ambiguity
  • Systematic diagnostic thinking
  • Hypothesis generation
  • Test design to distinguish between hypotheses
  • Recognition that environmental factors matter
  • Ability to create decision trees, not just linear troubleshooting
  • Knowing when you need more information
  • Having backup plans
  • Practical problem-solving (workarounds)

This is perhaps the most advanced diagnostic challenge because there's no single "right answer" - it's about the process and thinking, not reaching a predetermined conclusion.
